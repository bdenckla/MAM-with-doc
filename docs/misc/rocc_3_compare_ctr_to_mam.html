Comparing Psalm 32 in Chabad’s CTR to MAM

First let’s look at פשע in Psalm 32:1:

CTR, KCT, WMG נשוי־פֶּ֝֗שַׁע revia on pe & g. m. on pe
MAM נשוי־פֶּ֗שַׁע revia alone (no g. m.)
JP, SBB נשוי־פֶּ֝שַׁ֗ע revia on shin & g. m. on pe

All except MAM agree that the accent is revia mugrash.

In JP and SBB, the revia is on ש. All others have it on פ.

Side note: In CTR, פשע is accented with the CTR coding for geresh rather than geresh muqdam. (E.g., compare with the coding of בַּ֜֗ל and חֶ֜֗סֶד in 32:9 and 32:10.) Nonetheless, we interpret it as geresh muqdam.

Now let’s look at אשרי in Psalm 32:2:

CTR אַֽשְֽׁרֵ֥י אדם merkha on resh, space
MAM אַ֥שְֽׁרֵי־אדם merkha on alef, maqaf
KCT, SBB אַֽשְֽׁרֵי־אדם ga3ya on alef, maqaf
JP אַֽשְׁרֵ֥י אדם ≈ CTR
WMG אַשְׁרֵ֥י אדם ≈ CTR

(Note that we use “3” for the Hebrew letter ayin, following the convention of Arabizi, the Arabic chat alphabet. So, for example, געיה is transliterated as ga3ya rather than more conventional alternatives such as gaʿya.)

In JP and WMG, the shin has only shewa, not shewa with ga3ya as in the others. Generally, we will not mention differences like this, where one edition has ga3ya and another has neither ga3ya nor an accent. Such differences are interesting, but are less significant (and more widespread) than other types of differences.

In MAM, the maqaf after אשרי is marked up as a special kind of maqaf. This kind of maqaf is special because it (like many features of MAM) is an aid to reading that is not supplied by the authoritative manuscripts. (Other such features include qamats qatan and most non-pashta stress helpers.) For more information, including reference to Psalm 32:2 אשרי־אדם in particular, see the section “תיבה הראויה להיות מוקפת (מקף אפור)” in Chapter 2 of the MAM documentation.

Now let’s look at לא in Psalm 32:2:

CTR לֹֽא יחשב ga3ya without (!) maqaf
MAM, KCT, WMG לֹ֤א יחשב mahapakh
JP, SBB לֹֽא־יחשב ga3ya with maqaf

Now let’s look at כי in Psalm 32:3:

CTR, JP, SBB כִּ֣י החרשתי munaḥ
MAM, KCT, WMG כִּֽי־החרשתי ga3ya with maqaf

Now let’s look at יומם in Psalm 32:4 (we space the letters widely to avoid crowding):

CTR י֘וֹמָ֣ם munaḥ with tsinnorit (!)
MAM, KCT, WMG, SBB יוֹמָ֣ם munaḥ
JP י֘וֹמָ֤ם mahapakh with tsinnorit

The CTR edition illegally combines tsinnorit with munaḥ (A tsinnorit always and only precedes either a merkha or a mahapakh.) Perhaps this is an unfortunate mix of two traditions: a tradition of munaḥ (MAM and others) and a tradition of mahapakh with tsinnorit (JP).

Now let’s look at ולילה in Psalm 32:4: (using subscript “?” for ambiguous paseq/legarmeh)

CTR וָ֨לַיְלָה׀? vav (!) has azla
MAM, KCT, WMG, SBB וָלַיְלָה֮ tsinnor
JP azla floats between vav and lamed

In CTR and JP, the accent is an azla. (The azla is either alone or part of azla legarmeh, depending on whether we interpret the vertical line after this word as paseq or legarmeh.) In MAM and others, the accent is tsinnor.

The location of azla in CTR (on vav) seems likely to be an error. This error may stem from mistranscribing the floating azla in JP. It makes more sense to assign the floating azla to lamed than to vav. The only analogy we find in all of Tanakh is munaḥ on vav in Exodus 36:38 וְאֶת־וָ֣וֵיהֶ֔ם . The analogy is distant because munaḥ is not the primary accent on that word.

Now let’s look at what separates ולילה from the next word, תכבד, in Psalm 32:4 (using subscripts פ and ל for paseq and legarmeh):

CTR-A, JP-A, WMG ולילה ׀פ תכבד paseq
CTR-B, JP-B ולילה׀ל תכבד space
MAM, KCT, SBB ולילה תכבד space

In MAM and others, only a space separates ולילה from תכבד. In CTR and JP, there is a vertical line after ולילה, but it is hard to say whether in CTR that line should be interpreted as paseq or legarmeh. If we might interpret the line as legarmeh, it is the second mark of the two-mark accent azla legarmeh. In that case, we consider the words to be separated by only a space, because we consider the legarmeh to be part of the pointing of ולילה rather than part of what separates ולילה from תכבד.

Now let’s look at תכבד in Psalm 32:4:

CTR, JP תִּכְבַּ֤ד mahapakh
MAM, KCT, WMG, SBB תִּכְבַּ֥ד merkha

Now let’s look at ועוני in Psalm 32:5.This is the word that originally sparked my interest in Psalm 32 in CTR. (See my document, “Tsinnorit in Psalm 32:5 ועוני.”)

CTR וַֽעֲ֘וֺנִ֤י ayin (!) holds tsinnorit
MAM וַעֲוֺ֘נִ֤י vav #2 holds tsinnorit
SBB, KCT וַֽעֲוֺ֘נִ֤י ≈ MAM
JP vav #1 (!) holds tsinnorit
WMG וַֽעֲוֺנִ֤֘י nun (!) holds tsinnorit

Ayin is the least plausible of the three locations for tsinnorit. No accent ever appears on a vocal shewa that is not initial. (This is true regardless of whether that vocal shewa is notated as a simple shewa or a ḥataf shewa, i.e. a hataf vowel). (See my document, “Tsinnorit & Oleh on Initial Vocal Shewa.”)

In JP, there are two anomalies. (1) There are two dots above the nun, almost like an “above tsere,” if such a thing existed. Perhaps the first of these two dots is the ḥolam ḥaser dot of the vav, placed oddly late. But the second dot is inexplicable. (2) The ḥataf pataḥ of the ayin is oddly early. Such placement is needed when the “tail” of the ayin is a descender, but the tail of this ayin is not a descender: it is tucked to the side.

Side note: we represent WMG as if it had special placement for ḥolam ḥaser on vav, but it does not.

Now let’s look at נשאת in Psalm 32:5:

CTR נָשָׂ֤אתָ just mahapakh (no tsinnorit )
MAM, KCT, JP, WMG, SBB נָ֘שָׂ֤אתָ mahapakh with tsinnorit

Now let’s look at מים in Psalm 32:6 :

CTR, JP, SBB מַ֥יִם merkha
MAM, KCT, WMG מַ֣יִם munaḥ

Now let’s look at תהיו in Psalm 32:9:

CTR אל־תִּהְי֨וּ׀ azla legarmeh
MAM אל־תִּהְי֤וּ׀ mahapakh legarmeh
KCT, WMG, SBB אל־תִּֽהְי֤וּ׀ ≈ MAM
JP אל־תִּהִ֨יוּ׀ azla on he (!)
he has ḥiriq (!)

Now let’s look at אין in Psalm 32:9:

CTR, KCT, JP, WMG אֵ֢ין atnaḥ hafukh
MAM, SBB אֵ֤ין mahapakh

Even though the editions in question do not distinguish between atnaḥ hafukh and yeraḥ ben yomo, we know that atnaḥ hafukh is intended because it comes right before oleh veyored.

Now let’s look at במתג ורסן in Psalm 32:9:

CTR, JP בְּמֶ֣תֶג וָ֭רֶסֶן munaḥ, deḥi
MAM בְּמֶתֶג־וָרֶ֣סֶן maqaf, munaḥ
KCT, WMG, SBB בְּמֶֽתֶג־וָרֶ֣סֶן ≈ MAM

Most of the accent differences we’ve seen involve two conjunctive accents or involve a conjunctive accent and maqaf (the lack of an accent) (the formation of a compound word). But here we have a difference involving a disjunctive accent (deḥi ) and a conjunctive accent (munaḥ). A difference like this is generally considered to be a more significant difference than a conjunctive vs. conjunctive difference or a conjunctive vs. maqaf difference.

Now let’s look at קרב/קרוב in Psalm 32:9:

CTR קְר֣וֹב ḥolam malei
MAM, KCT, JP, WMG, SBB קְרֹ֣ב ḥolam ḥaser

Now let’s look at לרשע in Psalm 32:10:

CTR, KCT, JP, SBB לָֽרָ֫שָׁ֥ע oleh on resh
MAM לָ֫רָשָׁ֥ע oleh on lamed
WMG לָֽ֫רָשָׁ֥ע ≈ MAM

Now let’s look at שמחו in Psalm 32:11:

CTR, JP שִׂמְח֤וּ mahapakh
MAM, KCT, WMG, SBB שִׂמְח֬וּ iluy

Two other editions deserve some mention and comparison: the Jerusalem Crown (JC) and the Feldheim Simanim (FS) editions. They are so close to MAM that we have not listed them above. Yet, they differ from MAM in some small ways that are worth listing here.

MAM, FS אשרי־אדם maqaf
JC, AC, LC אשרי אדם space
MAM, JC, AC, LC כֽי־החרשתי ga3ya
FS כי־החרשתי no ga3ya
MAM ל֫רשע oleh on lamed
JC, FS, AC, LC לר֫שע oleh on resh

Some manuscripts and editions have a ga3ya where MAM has neither a ga3ya nor an accent:

JC, FS וֽעוני (absent in AC, LC, & MAM)
JC, FS, LC אל־תֽהיו (absent in AC & MAM)
JC, FS, LC במֽתג־ורסן (absent in AC & MAM)
JC, FS לֽרשע (absent in AC, LC, & MAM)

Regarding JC ... In the four cases of ga3ya above, JC uses a small ga3ya. This small ga3ya is an aid to reading that is not supplied by the AC. Thus one could say that in these words, JC sort of splits the difference between MAM and the many other editions that have a ga3ya (of full size).
