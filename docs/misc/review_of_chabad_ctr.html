Review of Chabad’s CTR

The Chabad website has an edition of the Hebrew Bible called The Complete Tanach with Rashi (CTR). (See my document, “On the Provenance of Chabad’s CTR.”)

This CTR is certainly the weirdest Hebrew Bible on the web, and possibly the worst. Whether it is the worst depends somewhat on (1) taste and (2) what purpose CTR would be put to.

For example, this CTR, though full of what most people would consider errors, might still be correct in some cases where other editions are wrong. In other words, its very weirdness, though usually a negative, might in some cases be a positive.

I will not attempt to give a comprehensive review of CTR. Instead, I will review its Psalm 32. If its Psalm 32 is representative, my review should give a good idea of CTR as a whole. At a minimum, my review is likely to give a good idea of of CTR’s poetic books, since some of CTR’s issues are specific to the poetic accents.

First, I will discuss the ways in which CTR’s Psalm 32 differs systematically from other editions. Then I will discuss the particular (i.e. non-systematic) ways in which CTR’s Psalm 32 differs from other editions.

Systematically, CTR’s Psalm 32 is restricted to the code points in Unicode 2.0. There are three code points that were introduced later that are used in some editions of Psalm 32:

  • QAMATS QATAN
  • ATNAH HAFUKH
  • HOLAM HASER FOR VAV

In the first two of the three cases, the fact that CTR does not use the newer code point causes no problems. Indeed many editions, even quite recent ones, are like CTR in that they do not make the following distinctions:

  • qamats vs. qamats qatan
  • yeraḥ ben yomo vs. atnaḥ hafukh

Both distinctions were introduced in Unicode 4.1, although ATNAH HAFUKH was introduced in a way that makes its adoption difficult, even to this day. See my proposal to the Unicode SEWG.

The third post-2.0 code point that CTR might have used is HOLAM HASER FOR VAV. In this case, the fact that CTR does not use it causes a problem. It causes a problem because CTR does not merely decline to make the distinction that this code point enables. Rather, CTR uses a nonstandard workaround to try to make this distinction. This workaround may work (accidentally or intentionally) in some fonts, but not in all fonts.

The workaround is to use the sequence ‹ZWJ, HOLAM› instead of the single code point HOLAM HASER FOR VAV. For example, to encode the ḥolam ḥaser on the letter vav in עוֺן in Psalm 32:5 (fully: עֲוֺ֖ן ), instead of HOLAM HASER FOR VAV, CTR uses ‹ZWJ, HOLAM›. In some fonts, this may accidentally have something like the desired appearance, since the ZWJ may “confuse” the font into putting the HOLAM fully after the vav. But in other fonts, the HOLAM will still appear directly over the vav, as it would if there were no ZWJ.

The CTR edition even declines to avail itself of distinctions that were available in Unicode 2.0. For example, CTR does not distinguish tsinnor from tsinnorit, using only the ZARQA code point for both. To be fair, the annotations and names of the ZARQA and ZINOR code points were, in my opinion, botched, and that could explain why CTR uses only ZARQA. According to the annotation for ZARQA, it should be used for both tsinnor and tsinnorit if an above-center (rather than above-left) placement of tsinnor is desired. In my opinion, this annotation encourages the use of Unicode to make a typographic choice that should be left up to fonts, not “baked in” to the encoding. (See my proposal to the Unicode SEWG.)

In addition to tsinnor and tsinnorit, there are three other pairs of accents where CTR declines to avail itself of distinctions that were available in Unicode 2.0:

  • The code point TIPEHA means either deḥi (!) or tarḥa / tipeḥa.
  • The code point GERESH means either geresh muqdam (!) or geresh.
  • The code point YETIV means either yetiv or mahapakh (!).

Each of these three pairs consists of a prepositive accent and an impositive “lookalike.” Sometimes CTR makes these distinctions in nonstandard ways, and sometimes it makes no distinction at all. (See my document, “Pre-vowel Accents in Chabad’s CTR.”)

Now let's move on from systematic differences to unsystematic ones. First let’s look at פשע in Psalm 32:1:

CTR פֶּ֝֗שַׁע
MAM פֶּ֗שַׁע
Warsaw CTR
Soncino פֶּ֝שַׁ֗ע
  • All except MAM agree that the accent is revia mugrash.
  • All except Soncino agree that the stress (or at least, the revia) is on פ.

Side note: In CTR, פשע is accented with the CTR coding for geresh rather than geresh muqdam. Nonetheless, we interpret it as geresh muqdam. (It would be willfully uncharitable to CTR to interpret it as geresh.)

Now let’s look at אשרי in Psalm 32:2:

CTR אַֽשְֽׁרֵ֥י
MAM אַ֥שְֽׁרֵי
Warsaw אַשְׁרֵ֥י
Soncino אַֽשְֽׁרֵי־
  • All except Soncino agree that the accent is merkha. Soncino has maqaf, which can be interpreted to mean “no accent.”
  • The CTR and Warsaw editions agree that the stress is on רי. MAM has the stress on א; Soncino has maqaf, so there is no primary stress, but Soncino’s gaʿya on א could be interpreted as calling for some kind of non-primary stress on א.
  • The editions differ in their use of gaʿya, but I will not discuss the details. I will not discuss them because although such differences are interesting, they are usually more a matter of taste than of correctness.

Now let’s look at לא in Psalm 32:2:

CTR לֹֽא
MAM לֹ֤א
Warsaw MAM
Soncino לֹֽא־
  • The MAM and Warsaw editions agree that the accent is mahapakh. The CTR and Soncino editions have no accent: they have only gaʿya.
  • The CTR edition is missing a maqaf, i.e. it should match the Soncino.

Now let’s look at כי in Psalm 32:3:

CTR כִּ֣י
MAM כִּֽי־
Warsaw MAM
Soncino CTR
  • The CTR and Soncino editions agree that the accent is munaḥ. The MAM and Warsaw editions have no accent: they have only gaʿya-maqaf.

Now let’s look at יומם in Psalm 32:4 (letters somewhat widely spaced to avoid crowding the details):

CTR י֘וֹמָ֣ם
MAM יוֹמָ֣ם
Warsaw MAM
Soncino MAM
  • All except CTR agree that yod is free of any accent. Extraordinarily (illegally!) CTR has tsinnorit on yod. (It is widely agreed that tsinnorit always and only precedes either merkha or mahapakh.)

Now let’s look at ולילה in Psalm 32:4:

CTR וָ֨לַיְלָה
MAM וָלַיְלָה֮
Warsaw MAM
Soncino MAM
  • All except CTR agree that the accent is tsinnor. In CTR, the accent is an improbable azla on vav. The only analogy I find in all of Tanakh is munaḥ on vav in Exodus 36:38 וְאֶת־וָ֣וֵיהֶ֔ם . The analogy is distant because munaḥ is not the primary accent on that word.

Now let’s look at what separates ולילה from the next word, תכבד, in Psalm 32:4:

CTR paseq or legarmeh
MAM space
Warsaw paseq
Soncino space
  • The MAM and Soncino editions agree that there is nothing other than a space between ולילה and תכבד. The CTR and Warsaw editions agree that there is a vertical line between these words, but it is hard to say whether in CTR that line should be interpreted as paseq or legarmeh. If we take seriously the improbable azla on vav in ולילה, then we might interpret the line as legarmeh, forming the two-mark (i.e. compound) accent azla-legarmeh.
