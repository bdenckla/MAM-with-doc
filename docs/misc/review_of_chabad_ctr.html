Review of Chabad’s CTR

Before starting this document, we note that we will use “3” for the Hebrew letter ayin, following the convention of Arabizi, the Arabic chat alphabet. So, for example, געיה is transliterated as ga3ya rather than more conventional alternatives such as gaʿya.

The Chabad website has an edition of the Hebrew Bible called The Complete Tanach with Rashi (CTR). (See my document, “On the Provenance of Chabad’s CTR.”)

This CTR is certainly the weirdest Hebrew Bible on the web, and possibly the worst. Whether it is the worst depends somewhat on (1) taste and (2) what purpose CTR would be put to.

For example, this CTR, though full of what most people would consider errors, might still be correct in some cases where other editions are wrong. In other words, its very weirdness, though usually a negative, might in some cases be a positive.

I will not attempt to give a comprehensive review of CTR. Instead, I will review its Psalm 32. If its Psalm 32 is representative, my review should give a good idea of CTR as a whole. At a minimum, my review is likely to give a good idea of of CTR’s poetic books, since some of CTR’s issues are specific to the poetic accents.

First, I will discuss the ways in which CTR’s Psalm 32 differs systematically from other editions. Then I will discuss the particular (i.e. non-systematic) ways in which CTR’s Psalm 32 differs from other editions.

Systematically, CTR’s Psalm 32 is restricted to the code points in Unicode 2.0. There are three code points that were introduced later that are used in some editions of Psalm 32:

  • QAMATS QATAN
  • ATNAH HAFUKH
  • HOLAM HASER FOR VAV

In the first two of the three cases, the fact that CTR does not use the newer code point causes no problems. Indeed many editions, even quite recent ones, are like CTR in that they do not make the following distinctions:

  • qamats vs. qamats qatan
  • yeraḥ ben yomo vs. atnaḥ hafukh

Both distinctions were introduced in Unicode 4.1, although ATNAH HAFUKH was introduced in a way that makes its adoption difficult, even to this day. See my proposal to the Unicode SEWG.

The third post-2.0 code point that CTR might have used is HOLAM HASER FOR VAV. In this case, the fact that CTR does not use it causes a problem. It causes a problem because CTR does not merely decline to make the distinction that this code point enables. Rather, CTR uses a nonstandard workaround to try to make this distinction. This workaround may work (accidentally or intentionally) in some fonts, but not in all fonts.

The workaround is the sequence ‹ZWJ, HOLAM›. For example, to encode the ḥolam ḥaser on the letter vav in עוֺן in Psalm 32:5 (fully: עֲוֺ֖ן ), instead of HOLAM HASER FOR VAV, CTR uses ‹ZWJ, HOLAM›. In some fonts, this may accidentally have something like the desired appearance, since the ZWJ may “confuse” the font into putting the HOLAM fully after the vav. But in other fonts, the HOLAM will still appear directly over the vav, as it would if there were no ZWJ.

The CTR edition even declines to avail itself of distinctions that were available in Unicode 2.0. For example, CTR does not distinguish tsinnor from tsinnorit, using only the ZARQA code point for both. To be fair, the annotations and names of the ZARQA and ZINOR code points were, in my opinion, botched, and that could explain why CTR uses only ZARQA. According to the annotation for ZARQA, it should be used for both tsinnor and tsinnorit if an above-center (rather than above-left) placement of tsinnor is desired. In my opinion, this annotation encourages the use of Unicode to make a typographic choice that should be left up to fonts, not “baked in” to the encoding. (See my proposal to the Unicode SEWG.)

In addition to tsinnor and tsinnorit, there are three other pairs of accents where CTR declines to avail itself of distinctions that were available in Unicode 2.0:

  • The code point TIPEHA means either deḥi (!) or tarḥa / tipeḥa.
  • The code point GERESH means either geresh muqdam (!) or geresh.
  • The code point YETIV means either yetiv or mahapakh (!).

Each of these three pairs consists of a prepositive accent and an impositive “lookalike.” Sometimes CTR makes these distinctions in nonstandard ways, and sometimes it makes no distinction at all. (See my document, “Pre-vowel Accents in Chabad’s CTR.”)

Now let's move on from systematic differences to unsystematic ones. We will compare CTR to four other editions:

  • Miqra al pi ha-Masorah (MAM)
  • Koren Classic Tanakh
  • Judaica Press Psalms (JP)

First let’s look at פשע in Psalm 32:1:

CTR, Koren נְֽשׂוּי־פֶּ֝֗שַׁע revia and g. m. on pe
MAM נְֽשׂוּי־פֶּ֗שַׁע revia alone (no g. m.)
JP נְֽשׂוּי־פֶּ֝שַׁ֗ע revia on shin
  • All except MAM agree that the accent is revia mugrash.
  • All except JP agree that the revia should be on פ.

Side note: In CTR, פשע is accented with the CTR coding for geresh rather than geresh muqdam. (E.g., compare with the coding of בַּ֜֗ל and חֶ֜֗סֶד in 32:9 and 32:10.) Nonetheless, we interpret it as geresh muqdam.

Now let’s look at אשרי in Psalm 32:2:

CTR אַֽשְֽׁרֵ֥י אָדָ֗ם merkha on resh, space
MAM אַ֥שְֽׁרֵי־אָדָ֗ם merkha on alef, maqaf
Koren אַֽשְֽׁרֵי־אָדָ֗ם ga3ya on alef, maqaf
JP אַֽשְׁרֵ֥י אָדָ֗ם ≈ CTR
  • In MAM and Koren, אשרי is not an independent word: it is the first part of a two-part compound word. Since the merkha in MAM is merely functioning as a secondary accent, the merkha vs. ga3ya difference between MAM and Koren is less significant than it would be in other contexts.
  • In JP, the shin has only shewa, not shewa with ga3ya as in the others. Generally, I will not mention differences like this, where one edition has ga3ya and another has neither ga3ya nor an accent. Such differences are interesting, but are less significant (and more widespread) than other types of differences.

In MAM, the maqaf after אשרי is marked up as a special kind of maqaf. This kind of maqaf is special because it (like many features of MAM) is an aid to reading that is not supplied by the authoritative manuscripts. (Other such features include qamats qatan and most non-pashta stress helpers.) For more information, including reference to Psalm 32:2 אשרי־אדם in particular, see the section “תיבה הראויה להיות מוקפת (מקף אפור)” in Chapter 2 of the MAM documentation.

Now let’s look at לא in Psalm 32:2:

CTR לֹֽא ga3ya without (!) maqaf
MAM, Koren לֹ֤א mahapakh
JP לֹֽא־ ga3ya with maqaf
  • In CTR and JP, there is no accent, only ga3ya. In MAM and Koren, the accent is mahapakh.
  • The CTR edition is missing a maqaf.

Now let’s look at כי in Psalm 32:3:

CTR, JP כִּ֣י munaḥ
MAM, Koren כִּֽי־ maqaf
  • In CTR and JP, the accent is munaḥ. In MAM and Koren, there is no accent, only ga3ya with maqaf.

Now let’s look at יומם in Psalm 32:4 (we space the letters widely to avoid crowding):

CTR י֘וֹמָ֣ם tsinnorit & munaḥ (!)
MAM, Koren יוֹמָ֣ם munaḥ
JP י֘וֹמָ֤ם tsinnorit & mahapakh
  • The CTR edition illegally combines tsinnorit with munaḥ. (A tsinnorit always and only precedes either a merkha or a mahapakh.) I speculate that this is an unfortunate mix of the traditions represented, on the one hand, by MAM and Koren (munaḥ), and on the other hand, by JP (tsinnorit & mahapakh, aka mahapakh metsunneret ).

Now let’s look at ולילה in Psalm 32:4: (using subscript “?” for ambiguous paseq/legarmeh)

CTR וָ֨לַיְלָה׀? vav (!) has azla
MAM, Koren וָלַיְלָה֮ tsinnor
JP azla floats between vav and lamed
  • In CTR and JP, the accent is an azla. (The azla is either alone or part of azla legarmeh, depending on whether we interpret the vertical line after this word as paseq or legarmeh.) In MAM and Koren, the accent is tsinnor.
  • The location of azla in CTR (on vav) seems likely to be an error. This error may stem from mistranscribing the floating azla in JP. It makes more sense to assign the floating azla to lamed than to vav. The only analogy I find in all of Tanakh is munaḥ on vav in Exodus 36:38 וְאֶת־וָ֣וֵיהֶ֔ם . The analogy is distant because munaḥ is not the primary accent on that word.

Now let’s look at what separates ולילה from the next word, תכבד, in Psalm 32:4 (using subscripts פ and ל for paseq and legarmeh):

CTR-A, JP-A ולילה ׀פ תכבד paseq
CTR-B, JP-B ולילה׀ל תכבד space
MAM, Koren ולילה תכבד space
  • In MAM, only a space separates ולילה from תכבד. In CTR and JP, there is a vertical line after ולילה, but it is hard to say whether in CTR that line should be interpreted as paseq or legarmeh. If we might interpret the line as legarmeh, it is the second mark of the two-mark accent azla legarmeh. In that case, we consider the words to be separated by only a space, because we consider the legarmeh to be part of the pointing of ולילה rather than part of what separates ולילה from תכבד.

Now let’s look at תכבד in Psalm 32:4:

CTR, JP תִּכְבַּ֤ד mahapakh
MAM, Koren תִּכְבַּ֥ד merkha

Now let’s look at ועוני in Psalm 32:5.This is the word that originally sparked my interest in Psalm 32 in CTR. (See my document, “Tsinnorit in Psalm 32:5 ועוני.”)

CTR וַֽעֲ֘וֺנִ֤י ayin (!) holds tsinnorit
MAM, Koren וַעֲוֺ֘נִ֤י vav #2 holds tsinnorit
JP vav #1 (!) holds tsinnorit
  • Ayin is the least plausible of the three locations for tsinnorit. Other than a prepositive, no accent ever appears on a vocal shewa, whether that shewa is notated as a simple shewa or a ḥataf shewa (a hataf vowel). (See my document, “Tsinnorit & Oleh on Initial Vocal Shewa.”)
  • In JP, there are two anomalies. (1) There are two dots above the nun, almost like an “above tsere,” if such a thing existed. Perhaps the first of these two dots is the ḥolam ḥaser dot of the vav, placed oddly late. But the second dot is inexplicable. (2) The ḥataf pataḥ of the ayin is oddly early. Such placement is needed when the “tail” of the ayin is a descender, but the tail of this ayin is not a descender: it is tucked to the side.

CTR נָשָׂ֤אתָ no tsinnorit, just mahapakh
MAM, Koren, JP נָ֘שָׂ֤אתָ mahapakh metsunneret
